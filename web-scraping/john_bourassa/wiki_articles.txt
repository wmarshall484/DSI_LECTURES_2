The unicorn was depicted in ancient seals of the <a href=\"/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Indus Valley Civilization\">Indus Valley Civilization</a> and was mentioned by the <a href=\"/wiki/Ancient_Greece\" title=\"Ancient Greece\">ancient Greeks</a> in accounts of <a href=\"/wiki/Natural_history\" title=\"Natural history\">natural history</a> by various writers, including <a href=\"/wiki/Ctesias\" title=\"Ctesias\">Ctesias</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Strabo\" title=\"Strabo\">Strabo</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger\" title=\"Pliny the Younger\">Pliny the Younger</a>, and <a href=\"/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus\" title=\"Claudius Aelianus\">Aelian</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Britannica_1-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Britannica-1\">[1]</a></sup> The <a href=\"/wiki/Bible\" title=\"Bible\">Bible</a> also describes an animal, the <a href=\"/wiki/Re%27em\" title=\"Re'em\">re'em</a>, which some versions translate as <i>unicorn</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Britannica_1-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Britannica-1\">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In European folklore, the unicorn is often depicted as a white horse-like or goat-like animal with a long horn and cloven hooves (sometimes a goat's beard). In the <a href=\"/wiki/Middle_Ages\" title=\"Middle Ages\">Middle Ages</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Renaissance\" title=\"Renaissance\">Renaissance</a>, it was commonly described as an extremely wild <a href=\"/wiki/Forest\" title=\"Forest\">woodland</a> creature, a symbol of purity and grace, which could only be captured by a virgin. In the encyclopedias its horn was said to have the power to render poisoned water potable and to heal sickness. In medieval and Renaissance times, the tusk of the <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a> was sometimes sold as unicorn horn.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id=\"toc\" class=\"toc\">\n<div href=\"/wiki/Greek_mythology\" title=\"Greek mythology\">Greek mythology</a>, but rather in the accounts of <a href=\"/wiki/Natural_history\" title=\"Natural history\">natural history</a>, for Greek writers of natural history were convinced of the reality of unicorns, which they located in <a href=\"/wiki/India\" title=\"India\">India</a>, a distant and fabulous realm for them. The earliest description is from <a href=\"/wiki/Ctesias\" title=\"Ctesias\">Ctesias</a>, who in his book <i><a href=\"/wiki/Indica_(Ctesias)\" title=\"Indica (Ctesias)\">Indika</a></i> (\"On <a href=\"/wiki/India\" title=\"India\">India</a>\") described them as <a href=\"/wiki/Onager\" title=\"Onager\">wild asses</a>, fleet of foot, having a horn a <a href=\"/wiki/Cubit\" title=\"Cubit\">cubit</a> and a half (700&#160;mm, 28 inches) in length, and colored white, red and black.<sup id=\"cite_ref-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-2\">[2]</a></sup> <a href=\"/wiki/Aristotle\" title=\"Aristotle\">Aristotle</a> must be following Ctesias when he mentions two one-horned animals, the <a href=\"/wiki/Oryx\" title=\"Oryx\">oryx</a> (a kind of <a href=\"/wiki/Antelope\" title=\"Antelope\">antelope</a>) and the so-called \"Indian ass\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-3\">[3]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-4\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-4\">[4]</a></sup> <a href=\"/wiki/Strabo\" title=\"Strabo\">Strabo</a> says that in the <a href=\"/wiki/Caucasus\" title=\"Caucasus\">Caucasus</a> there were one-horned horses with stag-like heads.<sup id=\"cite_ref-5\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-5\">[5]</a></sup> <a href=\"/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder\" title=\"Pliny the Elder\">Pliny the Elder</a> mentions the oryx and an Indian <a href=\"/wiki/Ox\" title=\"Ox\">ox</a> (perhaps a <a href=\"/wiki/Rhinoceros\" title=\"Rhinoceros\">rhinoceros</a>) as one-horned beasts, as well as \"a very fierce animal called the monoceros which has the head of the <a href=\"/wiki/Deer\" title=\"Deer\">stag</a>, the feet of the <a href=\"/wiki/Elephant\" title=\"Elephant\">elephant</a>, and the tail of the <a href=\"/wiki/Boar\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Boar\">boar</a>, while the rest of the body is like that of the horse; it makes a deep lowing noise, and has a single black horn, which projects from the middle of its forehead, two cubits [900&#160;mm, 35 inches] in length.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-6\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-6\">[6]</a></sup> In <i>On the Nature of Animals</i> (<i>\u03a0\u03b5\u03c1\u1f76 \u0396\u1ff4\u03c9\u03bd \u1f38\u03b4\u03b9\u03cc\u03c4\u03b7\u03c4\u03bf\u03c2</i>, <i>De natura animalium</i>), <a href=\"/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus\" title=\"Claudius Aelianus\">Aelian</a>, quoting Ctesias, adds that India produces also a one-horned horse (iii. 41; iv. 52),<sup id=\"cite_ref-7\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-7\">[7]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-8\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-8\">[8]</a></sup> and says (xvi. 20)<sup id=\"cite_ref-9\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-9\">[9]</a></sup> that the <i>monoceros</i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Greek_language\" title=\"Greek language\">Greek</a>: <span lang=\"el\" xml:lang=\"el\">\u03bc\u03bf\u03bd\u03cc\u03ba\u03b5\u03c1\u03c9\u03c2</span>) was sometimes called <i>cartazonos</i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Greek_language\" title=\"Greek language\">Greek</a>: <span lang=\"el\" xml:lang=\"el\">\u03ba\u03b1\u03c1\u03c4\u03ac\u03b6\u03c9\u03bd\u03bf\u03c2</span>), which may be a form of the Arabic <i><a href=\"/wiki/Karkadann\" title=\"Karkadann\">karkadann</a></i>, meaning \"<a href=\"/wiki/Rhinoceros\" title=\"Rhinoceros\">rhinoceros</a>\".</p>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Cosmas_Indicopleustes\" title=\"Cosmas Indicopleustes\">Cosmas Indicopleustes</a>, a merchant of <a href=\"/wiki/Alexandria\" title=\"Alexandria\">Alexandria</a> who lived in the 6th century, made a voyage to <a href=\"/wiki/India\" title=\"India\">India</a> and subsequently wrote works on <a href=\"/wiki/Cosmography\" title=\"Cosmography\">cosmography</a>. He gives a description of a unicorn based on four brass figures in the palace of the King of <a href=\"/wiki/Ethiopia\" title=\"Ethiopia\">Ethiopia</a>. When it finds itself pursued and in danger of capture, it throws itself from a precipice, and turns so aptly in falling, that it receives all the shock upon the horn, and so escapes safe and sound.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-10\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-10\">[10]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-11\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-11\">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A one-horned knowledge of the fabulous beast stemmed from <a href=\"/wiki/Re%27em\" title=\"Re'em\">biblical</a> and ancient sources, and the creature was variously represented as a kind of <a href=\"/wiki/Onager\" title=\"Onager\">wild ass</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Goat\" title=\"Goat\">goat</a>, or <a href=\"/wiki/Horse\" title=\"Horse\">horse</a>.</p>\n<p>The predecessor of the medieval <a href=\"/wiki/Bestiary\" title=\"Bestiary\">bestiary</a>, compiled in <a href=\"/wiki/Late_Antiquity\" title=\"Late Antiquity\">Late Antiquity</a> and known as <i><a href=\"/wiki/Physiologus\" title=\"Physiologus\">Physiologus</a></i> (<i>\u03a6\u03c5\u03c3\u03b9\u03bf\u03bb\u03cc\u03b3\u03bf\u03c2</i>), popularized an elaborate <a href=\"/wiki/Allegory\" title=\"Allegory\">allegory</a> in which a unicorn, trapped by a maiden (representing the <a href=\"/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mary (mother of Jesus)\">Virgin Mary</a>), stood for the <a href=\"/wiki/Incarnation\" title=\"Incarnation\">Incarnation</a>. As soon as the unicorn sees her, it lays its head on her lap and falls asleep. This became a basic emblematic tag that underlies medieval notions of the unicorn, justifying its appearance in every form of <a href=\"/wiki/Sacred_art\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Sacred art\">religious art.</a> Interpretations of the unicorn myth focus on the medieval lore of beguiled lovers,<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)\">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> whereas some religious writers interpret the unicorn and its death as the <a href=\"/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Passion (Christianity)\">Passion of Christ</a>. The myths refer to a beast with one horn that can only be tamed by a <a href=\"/wiki/Virginity\" title=\"Virginity\">virgin</a>; subsequently, some writers translated this into an allegory for Christ's relationship with the Virgin Mary.</p>\n<p>The unicorn also figured in <a href=\"/wiki/Courtly_love\" title=\"Courtly love\">courtly terms</a>: for some 13th-century <a href=\"/wiki/France\" title=\"France\">French</a> authors such as <a href=\"/wiki/Theobald_I_of_Navarre\" title=\"Theobald I of Navarre\">Thibaut of Champagne</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Richard_de_Fournival\" title=\"Richard de Fournival\">Richard de Fournival</a>, the lover is attracted to his lady as the unicorn is to the virgin. With the rise of <a href=\"/wiki/Renaissance_humanism\" title=\"Renaissance humanism\">humanism</a>, the unicorn also acquired more orthodox secular meanings, emblematic of chaste love and faithful marriage. It plays this role in <a href=\"/wiki/Petrarch\" title=\"Petrarch\">Petrarch</a>'s <i>Triumph of Chastity</i>, and on the reverse of <a href=\"/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca\" title=\"Piero della Francesca\">Piero della Francesca</a>'s portrait of Battista Strozzi, paired with that of her husband <a href=\"/wiki/Federico_da_Montefeltro\" title=\"Federico da Montefeltro\">Federico da Montefeltro</a> (painted <i>c</i> 1472-74), Bianca's triumphal car is drawn by a pair of unicorns.<sup id=\"cite_ref-14\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-14\">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href=\"/wiki/Throne_Chair_of_Denmark\" title=\"Throne Chair of Denmark\">Throne Chair of Denmark</a> is made of \"unicorn horns\" \u2013 almost certainly <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a> tusks. The same material was used for ceremonial cups because the unicorn's horn continued to be believed to neutralize poison, following classical authors.</p>\n<p>The unicorn, tamable only by a virgin woman, was well established in medieval lore by the time <a href=\"/wiki/Marco_Polo\" title=\"Marco Polo\">Marco Polo</a> described them as \"scarcely smaller than elephants. They have the hair of a buffalo and feet like an elephant's. They have a single large black horn in the middle of the forehead... They have a head like a wild boar's\u2026 They spend their time by preference wallowing in <a href=\"/wiki/Mud\" title=\"Mud\">mud</a> and slime. They are very ugly brutes to look at. They are not at all such as we describe them when we relate that they let themselves be captured by virgins, but clean contrary to our notions.\" It is clear that Marco Polo was describing a rhinoceros.<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)\">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href=\"/wiki/German_language\" title=\"German language\">German</a>, since the 16th century, <i>Einhorn</i> (\"one-horn\") has become a id=\"Alicorn\">Alicorn</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4\" title=\"Edit section: Alicorn\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role=\"note\" class=\"hatnote\">Main article: <a href=\"/wiki/Unicorn_horn\" title=\"Unicorn horn\">Unicorn horn</a></div>\n<p>The horn itself and the substance it was made of was called <b>alicorn</b>, and it was believed that the horn holds magical and medicinal properties. The <a href=\"/wiki/Danish_people\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Danish people\">Danish</a> physician <a href=\"/wiki/Ole_Worm\" title=\"Ole Worm\">Ole Worm</a> determined in 1638 that the alleged alicorns were the tusks of narwhals.<sup id=\"cite_ref-15\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-15\">[15]</a></sup> Such beliefs were examined wittily and at length in 1646 by Sir <a href=\"/wiki/Thomas_Browne\" title=\"Thomas Browne\">Thomas Browne</a> in his <i><a href=\"/wiki/Pseudodoxia_Epidemica\" title=\"Pseudodoxia Epidemica\">Pseudodoxia Epidemica</a></i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-16\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-16\">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>False alicorn powder, made from the tusks of <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhals</a> or horns of various animals, has been sold in Europe for medicinal purposes as late as 1741.<sup id=\"cite_ref-17\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-17\">[17]</a></sup> The alicorn was thought to cure many diseases and have the ability to detect poisons, and many physicians would make \"cures\" and sell them. Cups were made from alicorn for kings and given as a gift; these were usually made of <a href=\"/wiki/Ivory\" title=\"Ivory\">ivory</a> or <a href=\"/wiki/Walrus\" title=\"Walrus\">walrus</a> ivory. Entire horns were very precious in the Middle Ages and were often really the tusks of narwhals.<sup id=\"cite_ref-18\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-18\">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span Vinci\">Leonardo da Vinci</a> wrote:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The unicorn, through its intemperance and not knowing how to control itself, for the love it bears to fair maidens forgets its ferocity and wildness; and laying aside all fear it will go up to a seated damsel and go to sleep in her lap, and thus the hunters take it.<sup id=\"cite_ref-19\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-19\">[19]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The famous late <a href=\"/wiki/Gothic_art\" title=\"Gothic art\">Gothic</a> series of seven <a href=\"/wiki/Tapestry\" title=\"Tapestry\">tapestry</a> hangings <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Hunt_of_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Hunt of the Unicorn\">The Hunt of the Unicorn</a></i> are a high point in <a href=\"/wiki/Europe\" title=\"Europe\">European</a> tapestry manufacture, combining both secular and religious themes. The tapestries now hang in <a href=\"/wiki/The_Cloisters\" title=\"The Cloisters\">the Cloisters</a> division of the <a href=\"/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art\" title=\"Metropolitan Museum of Art\">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in <a href=\"/wiki/New_York_City\" title=\"New York City\">New York City</a>. In the series, richly dressed <a href=\"/wiki/Nobility\" title=\"Nobility\">noblemen</a>, accompanied by huntsmen and hounds, pursue a unicorn against <i><a href=\"/wiki/Mille-fleur\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mille-fleur\">mille-fleur</a></i> backgrounds or settings of buildings and gardens. They bring the animal to bay with the help of a maiden who traps it with her charms, appear to kill it, and bring it back to a castle; in the last and most famous panel, \"The Unicorn in Captivity\", the unicorn is shown alive again and happy, chained to a <a href=\"/wiki/Pomegranate\" title=\"Pomegranate\">pomegranate</a> tree surrounded by a fence, in a field of flowers. Scholars conjecture that the red stains on its flanks are not blood but rather the juice from pomegranates, which were a symbol of fertility. However, the true meaning of the mysterious resurrected unicorn in the last panel is unclear. The series was woven about 1500 in the <a href=\"/wiki/Low_Countries\" title=\"Low Countries\">Low Countries</a>, probably <a href=\"/wiki/Brussels\" title=\"Brussels\">Brussels</a> or <a href=\"/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Li\u00e8ge (city)\">Li\u00e8ge</a>, for an unknown patron. A set of six <a href=\"/wiki/Engraving\" title=\"Engraving\">engravings</a> on the same theme, treated rather differently, were engraved by the French artist <a href=\"/wiki/Jean_Duvet\" title=\"Jean Duvet\">Jean Duvet</a> in the 1540s.</p>\n<p>Another famous set of six tapestries of <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Lady_and_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Lady and the Unicorn\">Dame \u00e0 la licorne</a></i> (\"Lady with the unicorn\") in the <a href=\"/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_Cluny\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mus\u00e9e de Cluny\">Mus\u00e9e de Cluny</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Paris\" title=\"Paris\">Paris</a>, were also woven in the <a href=\"/wiki/Southern_Netherlands\" title=\"Southern Netherlands\">Southern Netherlands</a> before 1500, and show the five senses (the gateways to temptation) and finally Love (\"A mon seul desir\" the legend reads), with unicorns featured in each piece. Facsimiles of these unicorn tapestries are currently being woven for permanent display in <a href=\"/wiki/Stirling_Castle\" title=\"Stirling Castle\">Stirling Castle</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Scotland\" title=\"Scotland\">Scotland</a>, to take the place of a set recorded in the castle in a <a href=\"/wiki/Scottish_Royal_tapestry_collection\" title=\"Scottish Royal tapestry collection\">16th-century inventory</a>.</p>\n<p>A rather rare, late-15th-century, variant depiction of the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Hortus_conclusus\" title=\"Hortus conclusus\">hortus conclusus</a></i> in religious art combined the <a href=\"/wiki/Annunciation_to_Mary\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Annunciation to Mary\">Annunciation to Mary</a> with the themes of the <i>Hunt of the Unicorn</i> and <i>Virgin and Unicorn</i>, so popular in secular art. The unicorn already functioned as a symbol of the <a href=\"/wiki/Incarnation_of_Christ\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Incarnation of Christ\">Incarnation</a> and whether this meaning is intended in many <i>prima facie</i> secular depictions can be a difficult matter of scholarly interpretation. There is no such ambiguity in the scenes where the archangel <a href=\"/wiki/Gabriel\" title=\"Gabriel\">Gabriel</a> is shown blowing a horn, as hounds chase the unicorn into the Virgin's arms, and a little Christ Child descends on rays of light from God the Father. The <a href=\"/wiki/Council_of_Trent\" title=\"Council of Trent\">Council of Trent</a> finally banned this somewhat over-elaborated, if charming, depiction,<sup id=\"cite_ref-20\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-20\">[20]</a></sup> partly on the grounds of realism, as no one now believed the unicorn to be a real animal.</p>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Shakespeare\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shakespeare\">Shakespeare</a> scholars describe unicorns being captured by a hunter standing in front of a tree, the unicorn goaded into charging; the hunter would step aside the last moment and the unicorn would embed its horn deeply into the tree (See annotations<sup id=\"cite_ref-21\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-21\">[21]</a></sup> of <a href=\"/wiki/Timon_of_Athens\" title=\"Timon of Athens\">Timon of Athens</a>, Act 4, scene 3, c. line 341: \"wert thou the unicorn, pride and wrath would confound thee and make thine own self the conquest of thy fury\".</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Heraldry\">Heraldry</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6\" title=\"Edit section: Heraldry\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href=\"/wiki/Heraldry\" title=\"Heraldry\">heraldry</a>, a unicorn is often depicted as a horse with a goat's cloven hooves and beard, a lion's tail, and a slender, spiral horn on its forehead<sup id=\"cite_ref-friar_22-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-friar-22\">[22]</a></sup> (non-equine attributes may be replaced with equine ones, as can be seen from the following gallery). Whether because it was an emblem of the Incarnation or of the fearsome animal passions of raw nature, the unicorn was not widely used in early heraldry, but became popular from the 15th century.<sup id=\"cite_ref-friar_22-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-friar-22\">[22]</a></sup> Though sometimes shown collared and chained, which may be taken as an indication that it has been tamed or tempered, it is more usually shown collared with a broken chain attached, showing that it has broken free from its bondage.</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Scotland\">Scotland</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7\" title=\"Edit section: Scotland\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role=\"note\" class=\"hatnote\">See also: <a href=\"/wiki/The_Lion_and_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Lion and the Unicorn\">The Lion and the Unicorn</a></div>\n<p>In heraldry the unicorn is best known as the symbol of <a href=\"/wiki/Scotland\" title=\"Scotland\">Scotland</a>. The unicorn was chosen because it was seen as a proud and haughty beast which would rather die than be captured, just as Scots would fight to remain sovereign and unconquered.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Nisbet_23-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Nisbet-23\">[23]</a></sup> Two unicorns supported the <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_arms_of_Scotland\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Royal arms of Scotland\">royal arms</a> of the King of Scots, and since the <a href=\"/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707\" title=\"Acts of Union 1707\">1707 union</a> of England and Scotland, the <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_arms_of_the_United_Kingdom\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Royal arms of the United Kingdom\">royal arms of the United Kingdom</a> have been supported by a unicorn along with an English lion. Two versions of the royal arms exist: that used in Scotland gives more emphasis to the Scottish elements, placing the unicorn on the left and giving it a crown, whereas the version used in England and elsewhere gives the English elements more prominence.</p>\n<p>Golden coins known as the <a href=\"/wiki/Unicorn_(coin)\" title=\"Unicorn (coin)\">unicorn</a> and half-unicorn, both with a unicorn on the <a href=\"/wiki/Obverse\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Obverse\">obverse</a>, were used in Scotland in the 15th and 16th century. In the same realm, carved unicorns were often used as finials on the pillars of <a href=\"/wiki/Mercat_cross\" title=\"Mercat cross\">Mercat crosses</a>, and denoted that the settlement was a <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_burgh\" title=\"Royal burgh\">royal burgh</a>. Certain noblemen such as the <a href=\"/wiki/Earl_of_Kinnoull\" title=\"Earl of Kinnoull\">Earl of Kinnoull</a> were given special permission to use the unicorn in their arms, as an <a href=\"/wiki/Augmentation_of_honour\" title=\"Augmentation of honour\">augmentation of honour</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Nisbet_23-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Nisbet-23\">[23]</a></sup> The crest for <a href=\"/wiki/Clan_Cunningham\" title=\"Clan Cunningham\">Clan Cunningham</a> bears a unicorn head.<sup id=\"cite_ref-24\" class=\"reference\"><a class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9\" title=\"Edit section: Possible origins\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Hunts for an actual animal as the basis of the unicorn myth, accepting the conception of writers in Antiquity that it really existed somewhere at the edge of the known earth, have added a further layer of <a href=\"/wiki/Mythology\" title=\"Mythology\">mythologizing</a> about the unicorn. These have taken various forms, interpreted in a scientific, rather than a wonder-filled manner, to accord with modern perceptions of reality.</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Fabricated_evidence\">Fabricated evidence</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10\" title=\"Edit section: Fabricated evidence\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\">\n<div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Einhorn-Guericke.jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Einhorn-Guericke.jpg/220px-Einhorn-Guericke.jpg\" width=\"220\" height=\"259\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Einhorn-Guericke.jpg/330px-Einhorn-Guericke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/Einhorn-Guericke.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"400\" data-file-height=\"470\" /></a>\n<div class=\"thumbcaption\">\n<div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Einhorn-Guericke.jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>\n<a href=\"/wiki/Otto_von_Guericke\" title=\"Otto von Guericke\">Otto von Guericke</a>'s unicorn skeleton, exhibit near the Zoo, <a href=\"/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck\" title=\"Osnabr\u00fcck\">Osnabr\u00fcck</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Among numerous finds of <a href=\"/wiki/Prehistory\" title=\"Prehistory\">prehistoric</a> bones found at <a href=\"/wiki/Unicorn_Cave\" title=\"Unicorn Cave\">Unicorn Cave</a> in <a href=\"/wiki/Germany\" title=\"Germany\">Germany</a>'s <a href=\"/wiki/Harz\" title=\"Harz\">Harz Mountains</a>, some were selected and reconstructed by the mayor of <a href=\"/wiki/Magdeburg\" title=\"Magdeburg\">Magdeburg</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Otto_Von_Guericke\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Otto Von Guericke\">Otto Von Guericke</a>, as a unicorn in 1663 (<i>illustration, right</i>). Guericke's so-called unicorn had only two legs, and was constructed from <a href=\"/wiki/Fossil\" title=\"Fossil\">fossil</a> bones of a <a href=\"/wiki/Woolly_rhinoceros\" title=\"Woolly rhinoceros\">woolly rhinoceros</a> and a <a href=\"/wiki/Mammoth\" title=\"Mammoth\">mammoth</a>, with the horn of a <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a>. The skeleton was examined by <a href=\"/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Gottfried Leibniz\">Gottfried Leibniz</a>, who had previously doubted the existence of the unicorn, but was convinced by it.<sup id=\"cite_ref-25\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-25\">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Georges_Cuvier\" title=\"Georges Cuvier\">Baron Georges Cuvier</a> maintained that, as the unicorn was cloven-hoofed, it must therefore have a cloven skull (making the growth of a single horn impossible); as if to disprove this, Dr. <a href=\"/wiki/W._Franklin_Dove\" title=\"W. Franklin Dove\">W. Franklin Dove</a>, a <a href=\"/wiki/University_of_Maine\" title=\"University of Maine\">University of Maine</a> professor, artificially fused the horn buds of a <a href=\"/wiki/Calf_(animal)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Calf (animal)\">calf</a> together, creating the external appearance of a one-horned bull.<sup id=\"cite_ref-26\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-26\">[26]</a></sup><sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement\" title=\"Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute\"><span title=\"The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2016)\">dubious</span></a> <span class=\"metadata\">\u2013 <a href=\"/wiki/Talk:Unicorn#Dubious\" title=\"Talk:Unicorn\">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Unicorn_seals_of_the_Indus_Valley_Civilization\">Unicorn seals of the Indus Valley Civilization</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11\" title=\"Edit section: Unicorn seals of the Indus Valley Civilization\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tleft\">\n<div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:382px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg/380px-IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg\" width=\"380\" height=\"107\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg/570px-IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg/760px-IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"1482\" data-file-height=\"417\" /></a>\n<div class=\"thumbcaption\">\n<div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:IndusValleySeals_unicorns.jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>\nIndus valley seals showing 'unicorns' (<a href=\"/wiki/British_Museum\" title=\"British Museum\">British Museum</a>)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The first objects unearthed from <a href=\"/wiki/Harappa\" title=\"Harappa\">Harappa</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Mohenjo-Daro\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mohenjo-Daro\">Mohenjo-Daro</a>, major sites of the <a href=\"/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Indus Valley Civilization\">Indus Valley Civilization</a>, were small stone <a href=\"/wiki/Seal_(device)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Seal (device)\">seals</a> inscribed with elegant depictions of animals, including a unicorn-like figure, and marked with <a href=\"/wiki/Indus_script\" title=\"Indus script\">Indus script</a> writing which still baffles scholars. These seals are dated back to 2500 B.C.<sup id=\"cite_ref-27\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-27\">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The \"unicorn\" figures on the seals have been interpreted as representations of <a href=\"/wiki/Aurochs\" title=\"Aurochs\">aurochs</a>\u2014a type of large wild cattle that formerly inhabited Europe, Asia and North Africa\u2014or derivatives of aurochs. It is suggested that as the animal is always shown in profile, only one of the two horns is seen.<sup id=\"cite_ref-28\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-28\">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Elasmotherium_or_rhinoceros\"><i>Elasmotherium</i> or rhinoceros</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12\" title=\"Edit section: Elasmotherium or rhinoceros\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\">\n<div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Elasmotherium_model.jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Elasmotherium_model.jpg/220px-Elasmotherium_model.jpg\" width=\"220\" height=\"165\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Elasmotherium_model.jpg/330px-Elasmotherium_model.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Elasmotherium_model.jpg/440px-Elasmotherium_model.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"3648\" data-file-height=\"2736\" /></a>\n<div class=\"thumbcaption\">\n<div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Elasmotherium_model.jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>\nThe <a href=\"/wiki/Elasmotherium\" title=\"Elasmotherium\">elasmotherium</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>One suggestion is that the unicorn is based on the extinct rhinocerus species <i><a href=\"/wiki/Elasmotherium\" title=\"Elasmotherium\">Elasmotherium</a></i>, a huge <a href=\"/wiki/Eurasia\" title=\"Eurasia\">Eurasian</a> mammal native to the <a href=\"/wiki/Steppe\" title=\"Steppe\">steppes</a>, south of the range of the woolly rhinoceros of Ice Age Europe. <i>Elasmotherium</i> looked little like a horse, but it had a large single horn in its forehead. It became extinct about the same time as the rest of the glacial age <a href=\"/wiki/Megafauna\" title=\"Megafauna\">megafauna</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-29\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-29\">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>However, according to the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Nordisk_familjebok\" title=\"Nordisk familjebok\">Nordisk familjebok</a> (Nordic Familybook)</i> and science writer <a href=\"/wiki/Willy_Ley\" title=\"Willy Ley\">Willy Ley</a> the animal may have survived long enough to be remembered in the legends of the native European peoples as a huge black bull with a single horn in the forehead.</p>\n<p>In support of this claim, it has been noted that the 13th century traveller <a href=\"/wiki/Marco_Polo\" title=\"Marco Polo\">Marco Polo</a> claimed to have seen a unicorn in <a href=\"/wiki/Java\" title=\"Java\">Java</a>, but his description makes it clear to the modern reader that he actually saw a <a href=\"/wiki/Javan_rhinoceros\" title=\"Javan rhinoceros\">Javan rhinoceros</a>.</p>\n<p>More recent findings seem to place humans and the <a href=\"/wiki/Siberia\" title=\"Siberia\">Siberian</a> <i>elasmotherium</i> in the same area at the same time.<sup id=\"cite_ref-IBT_30-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-IBT-30\">[30]</a></sup> The fragmentary skull of an <i>Elasmotherium sibiricum</i> was found in <a href=\"/wiki/Kazakhstan\" title=\"Kazakhstan\">Kazakhstan</a> and carbon-dated in 2015 as ca. 30,000 years old.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Shpansky16_31-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Shpansky16-31\">[31]</a></sup> Skulls of <a href=\"/wiki/Anatomically_modern_human\" title=\"Anatomically modern human\">modern humans</a> found in the same area date back up to 45,000 years, this meaning that our species and this type of mammal possibly coexisted in Western Siberia for thousands of years.<sup id=\"cite_ref-IBT_30-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-IBT-30\">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Single-horned_goat\">Single-horned goat</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13\" vision of Daniel:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>And as I was considering, behold, a he-goat came from the west over the face of the whole earth, and touched not the ground: and the goat had a notable horn between his eyes. (<a rel=\"nofollow\" class=\"external text\" href=\"http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Daniel+8:5\u20138:5&amp;version=nrsv\">Daniel 8:5</a>)</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Modern_paganism\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Modern paganism\">Neo-Pagan</a> antiquities researcher <a href=\"/wiki/Oberon_Zell-Ravenheart\" title=\"Oberon Zell-Ravenheart\">Timothy Zell</a> and his wife <a href=\"/wiki/Morning_Glory_Zell-Ravenheart\" title=\"Morning Glory Zell-Ravenheart\">Morning Glory</a> also produced artificial unicorns dubbed \"the Living Unicorn\", remodelling the \"horn buds\" of goat kids in such a way that their horns grew together into a single one.<sup id=\"cite_ref-32\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-32\">[32]</a></sup> Zell theorized that this process might have been used in the past to create court curiosities and natural herd leaders, because the goat was able to use this long straight horn effectively as a weapon and a tool. <a href=\"/wiki/Medieval_art\" title=\"Medieval art\">Medieval art</a> often depicts unicorns as small, with cloven hooves and beards, sometimes resembling goats more than horses with horns. This process is possible only with animals that naturally have horns. For a time, a few of these unicorns travelled with the <a href=\"/wiki/Ringling_Brothers_Circus\" title=\"Ringling Brothers Circus\">Ringling Brothers Circus</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-33\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-33\">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"The_narwhal\">The narwhal</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span title=\"Cabinet of curiosities\">cabinets of curiosities</a> and other contexts in Medieval and <a href=\"/wiki/Renaissance\" title=\"Renaissance\">Renaissance</a> Europe, were very often examples of the distinctive straight spiral single tusk of the <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a> (<i>Monodon monoceros</i>), an <a href=\"/wiki/Arctic_Ocean\" title=\"Arctic Ocean\">Arctic</a> <a href=\"/wiki/Cetacea\" title=\"Cetacea\">cetacean</a>, as <a href=\"/wiki/Denmark\" title=\"Denmark\">Danish</a> zoologist <a href=\"/wiki/Ole_Worm\" title=\"Ole Worm\">Ole Worm</a> established in 1638.<sup id=\"cite_ref-34\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-34\">[34]</a></sup> They were brought south as a very valuable trade, and sold as horns from the legendary unicorn; being of <a href=\"/wiki/Ivory\" title=\"Ivory\">ivory</a>, they passed the various tests intended to spot fake unicorn horns.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Daston_35-0\" class=\"reference\"><a class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-36\">[36]</a></sup> The usual depiction of the spiral unicorn horn in art, derives from these.</p>\n<p>The truth of the tusk's origin developed gradually during the Age of Exploration, as explorers and naturalists began to visit regions themselves. In 1555, <a href=\"/wiki/Olaus_Magnus\" title=\"Olaus Magnus\">Olaus Magnus</a> published a drawing of a fish-like creature with a \"horn\" on its forehead.</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"The_oryx\">The oryx</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15\" title=\"Edit section: The oryx\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\">\n<div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Oryx_gazella_(unicorn).jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg/220px-Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg\" width=\"220\" height=\"147\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg/330px-Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg/440px-Oryx_gazella_%28unicorn%29.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"3000\" data-file-height=\"2000\" /></a>\n<div class=\"thumbcaption\">\n<div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Oryx_gazella_(unicorn).jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>\nOne-horned <a href=\"/wiki/Oryx\" title=\"Oryx\">oryx</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href=\"/wiki/Oryx\" title=\"Oryx\">oryx</a> is an <a href=\"/wiki/Antelope\" title=\"Antelope\">antelope</a> with two long, thin horns projecting from its forehead. Some have suggested that seen from the side and from a distance, the oryx looks something like a horse with a single horn (although the 'horn' projects backward, not forward as in the classic unicorn). Conceivably, travellers in <a href=\"/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula\" title=\"Arabian Peninsula\">Arabia</a> could have derived the tale of the unicorn from these animals. However, classical authors seem to distinguish clearly between oryxes and unicorns. The <i><a href=\"/wiki/Peregrinatio_in_terram_sanctam\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Peregrinatio in terram sanctam\">Peregrinatio in terram sanctam</a></i>, published in 1486, was the first printed illustrated travel-book, describing a <a href=\"/wiki/Pilgrimage\" title=\"Pilgrimage\">pilgrimage</a> to <a href=\"/wiki/Jerusalem\" title=\"Jerusalem\">Jerusalem</a>, and thence to <a href=\"/wiki/Egypt\" title=\"Egypt\">Egypt</a> by way of <a href=\"/wiki/Mount_Sinai\" title=\"Mount Sinai\">Mount Sinai</a>. It featured many large <a href=\"/wiki/Woodcut\" title=\"Woodcut\">woodcuts</a> by <a href=\"/wiki/Erhard_Reuwich\" title=\"Erhard Reuwich\">Erhard Reuwich</a>, who went on the trip, mostly detailed and accurate views of cities. The book also contained pictures of animals seen on the journey, including a <a href=\"/wiki/Crocodile\" title=\"Crocodile\">crocodile</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Camel\" title=\"Camel\">camel</a>, and unicorn\u2014presumably an oryx, which they could easily have seen on their route.</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"The_eland\">The eland</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16\" title=\"Edit section: The eland\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\">\n<div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Eland002.jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Eland002.jpg/220px-Eland002.jpg\" width=\"220\" height=\"165\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Eland002.jpg/330px-Eland002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Eland002.jpg/440px-Eland002.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"640\" data-file-height=\"480\" /></a>\n<div class=\"thumbcaption\">\n<div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Eland002.jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>\nThe <a href=\"/wiki/Common_eland\" title=\"Common eland\">eland</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In <a href=\"/wiki/Southern_Africa\" title=\"Southern Africa\">Southern Africa</a>, the <a href=\"/wiki/Common_eland\" title=\"Common eland\">eland</a> has somewhat mystical or spiritual connotations, perhaps at least partly because this very large antelope will defend itself against lions and is able to kill these fearsome predators. Eland are very frequently depicted in the <a href=\"/wiki/Rock_art\" title=\"Rock art\">rock art</a> of the region, which implies that they were viewed as having a strong connection to the other world, and in several languages the word for eland and for dance is the same; significant because shamans used dance as their means of drawing power from the other world. Eland fat was used when mixing the pigments for these pictographs, and in the preparation of many medicines.</p>\n<p>This special regard for the eland may well have been picked up by early travellers. There is a purported unicorn horn in the castle of the chief of the <a href=\"/wiki/Clan_MacLeod\" title=\"Clan MacLeod\">Clan MacLeod</a> in Scotland, which has been identified as that of an eland.<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)\">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Genetic_disorders_of_horned_or_antlered_animals\">Genetic disorders of horned or antlered animals</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17\" title=\"Edit section: Genetic disorders of horned or antlered animals\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A new possibility for the inspiration of the unicorn came in 2008 with the discovery of a <a href=\"/wiki/Roe_deer\" title=\"Roe deer\">roe deer</a> in <a href=\"/wiki/Italy\" title=\"Italy\">Italy</a> with a single antler. However, it is more accurately described as a <a href=\"/wiki/Category:Mythological_hybrids\" title=\"Category:Mythological hybrids\">hybrid animal</a> that looks less unicorn than <a href=\"/wiki/Chimera_(mythology)\" title=\"Chimera (mythology)\">chimera</a>, with the body of a deer, the head of a lion, green <a href=\"/wiki/Scale_(zoology)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Scale (zoology)\">scales</a> and a long forwardly-curved horn. The <a href=\"/wiki/Japanese_mythology\" title=\"Japanese mythology\">Japanese</a> version (<i>kirin</i>) more closely resembles the Western unicorn, even though it is based on the Chinese <i>qilin</i>. The Qu\u1ebb Ly of <a href=\"/wiki/Vietnam\" title=\"Vietnam\">Vietnamese</a> myth, similarly sometimes mistranslated \"unicorn\" is a symbol of wealth and prosperity that made its first appearance during the Duong Dynasty, about 600 CE, to Emperor Duong Cao To, after a military victory which resulted in his conquest of <a href=\"/wiki/T%C3%A2y_Nguy%C3%AAn\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"T\u00e2y Nguy\u00ean\">T\u00e2y Nguy\u00ean</a>. In November 2012 the History Institute of the DPRK Academy of Social Sciences, as well as the <a href=\"/wiki/Korean_Central_News_Agency\" title=\"Korean Central News Agency\">Korea News Service</a>, reported that the <a href=\"/wiki/Kiringul\" title=\"Kiringul\">Kiringul</a> had been found, which is associated with a kirin ridden by <a href=\"/wiki/King_Dongmyeong_of_Goguryeo\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"King Dongmyeong of Goguryeo\">King Dongmyeong of Goguryeo</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-41\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-41\">[41]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-42\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-42\">[42]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Beginning in the <a href=\"/wiki/Ming_Dynasty\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Ming Dynasty\">Ming Dynasty</a>, the <i>qilin</i> became associated with <a href=\"/wiki/Giraffe\" title=\"Giraffe\">giraffes</a>, after <a href=\"/wiki/Zheng_He\" title=\"Zheng He\">Zheng He</a>'s <a href=\"/wiki/Treasure_voyages\" title=\"Treasure voyages\">voyage</a> to <a href=\"/wiki/East_Africa\" title=\"East Africa\">East Africa</a> brought a pair of the long-necked animals and introduced them at court in <a href=\"/wiki/Nanjing\" title=\"Nanjing\">Nanjing</a> as <i>qilin</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-43\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-43\">[43]</a></sup> The resemblance to the <i>qilin</i> was noted in the giraffe's <a href=\"/wiki/Ossicones\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Ossicones\">ossicones</a> (bony protrusions from the skull resembling horns), graceful movements, and peaceful demeanor.<sup id=\"cite_ref-44\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-44\">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Shanhaijing\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shanhaijing\">Shanhaijing</a> (117) also mentioned <i>Bo</i>-horse (<a href=\"/wiki/Chinese_language\" title=\"Chinese language\">Chinese</a>: <span lang=\"zh\" xml:lang=\"zh\">\u99ee\u99ac</span>; <a href=\"/wiki/Pinyin\" title=\"Pinyin\">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang=\"zh-Latn-pinyin\" xml:lang=\"zh-Latn-pinyin\">b\u00f3m\u01ce</span></i>), a chimera horse with ox tail, single horn, white body, and its sound like person calling. The creature is lived at Honest-head Mountain. <a href=\"/wiki/Guo_Pu\" title=\"Guo Pu\">Guo Pu</a> in his <i>jiangfu</i> said that <i>Bo</i>-horse able to walk on water. Another similar creature also mentioned in <a href=\"/wiki/Shanhaijing\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shanhaijing\">Shanhaijing</a> (80) to live in Mount Winding-Centre as <i>Bo</i> (<a
